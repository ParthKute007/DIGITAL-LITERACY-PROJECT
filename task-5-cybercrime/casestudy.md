## Topic: UPI / Online Payment Fraud

### What is UPI Payment Fraud?

UPI (Unified Payments Interface) fraud is a form of financial cybercrime in which an attacker tricks a victim into authorising an illegal money transfer from their bank account. The attacker exploits the victim's unfamiliarity with how UPI payment requests work, using social engineering rather than any technical hacking. It is one of the fastest-growing cybercrimes in India, particularly targeting students, first-time digital payment users, and people selling items on online marketplaces.

### How It Typically Happens (Step-by-Step)

1. **Contact:** The attacker reaches the victim via a phone call, WhatsApp message, or SMS, posing as a bank representative, customer care agent, or an interested buyer on an OLX-style marketplace.
2. **Pretext:** The attacker creates urgency — for example, claiming the victim has won a cashback prize, that their account will be blocked unless they verify details, or that they want to pay for a listed item.
3. **Send a UPI collect request:** The attacker sends a payment *collect request* through a UPI app (PhonePe, Google Pay, Paytm), falsely framing it as a "verification step" or "money transfer to receive payment."
4. **Victim approves the request:** The victim enters their UPI PIN believing they are *receiving* money — but they are actually *sending* money directly to the attacker's account.
5. **Instant, irreversible transfer:** UPI transactions complete within seconds and cannot be reversed once approved. The money is gone immediately.

### Who Is Usually Targeted

- First-year college students and first-time UPI users
- Students or families selling second-hand items on OLX, Facebook Marketplace, or Quikr
- Job seekers who receive fake "advance salary" collect requests from fraudulent recruiters
- Anyone unfamiliar with the critical difference between "collecting" and "approving" on UPI apps

### Consequences

Victims typically lose between ₹500 and ₹50,000 or more in a single incident. Beyond financial loss, there is significant emotional distress, embarrassment, and long-term anxiety around digital payments. Many victims — especially students — do not report the crime due to fear of parental reaction or shame, which allows fraudsters to continue targeting others unchallenged. In some cases, students have taken loans or borrowed money from friends to cover the loss, creating additional financial stress during an already challenging academic period.
